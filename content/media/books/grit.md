# Grit

Angela Duckworth's *Grit: The Power of Passion and Perseverance* (2016). Rated 4/5, finished 2021-11-25. The Notion page held a long Readwise-synced Kindle highlight dump plus a linked video lecture; what follows is a curated synthesis of the themes.

## The core thesis

> It was this combination of passion and perseverance that made high achievers special. In a word, they had **grit**. *(Location 177)*

Duckworth studied West Point cadets, National Spelling Bee finalists, rookie teachers in tough schools, and business salespeople. Across domains, the predictor of who persisted was not talent — it was **grit**: sustained, long-term commitment to a consistent top-level goal, pursued through setbacks.

> **Our potential is one thing. What we do with it is quite another.** *(Location 261)*

## Duckworth's formulas

- **Talent × effort = skill.**
- **Skill × effort = achievement.**
- Effort therefore appears *twice*. Grit is the willingness to keep applying effort — a multiplier on both terms.

## Darwin's opinion

> "For I have always maintained that, excepting fools, men did not differ much in intellect, only in zeal and hard work; and I still think this is an eminently important difference." — Charles Darwin

William James, too: "Compared with what we ought to be, we are only half awake… the plain fact remains that men the world over possess amounts of resource which only very exceptional individuals push to their extremes."

## The Enron contrast

The McKinsey "War for Talent" era — talent-as-IQ — produced, at Enron, a culture of **unusually smart but unusually ungritty** employees. Talent alone, uncoupled from perseverance, is brittle.

## Four components of grit (Duckworth's hierarchy)

1. **Interest** — enjoyment of what you do at the deep-practice level.
2. **Practice** — deliberate, daily, kaizen-style improvement.
3. **Purpose** — the conviction that the work matters for others too.
4. **Hope** — "rising eight times after falling seven"; growth mindset; learned optimism.

## Parenting the gritty

A key section of the book — extracted from Larry Steinberg's ~10,000-teen study — classifies parenting on two axes:

### The matrix

|                | **Low demanding**   | **High demanding**      |
|----------------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| **High supportive** | Permissive          | **Wise / Authoritative** |
| **Low supportive**  | Neglectful          | Authoritarian            |

The **wise parent** is high in both dimensions: warm + respectful + demanding. This quadrant predicts the grittiest children.

### Sample items from the questionnaire

**Supportive: Warm**
- I can count on my parents to help me out if I have a problem.
- My parents spend time just talking to me.
- My parents and I do things that are fun together.

**Supportive: Respectful**
- My parents believe I have a right to my own point of view.
- My parents respect my privacy.

**Demanding**
- My parents really expect me to follow family rules.
- My parents point out ways I could do better.
- My parents expect me to do my best even when it's hard.

## Takeaways for the vault

- **Grit > talent** as a predictor of long-term achievement.
- **Effort is counted twice** — it builds the skill *and* it converts skill into achievement.
- **Parenting matrix** (warm × respectful × demanding) — transportable beyond child-rearing to mentoring and management.
- Pair with [[atomic-habits]] (the *how* of daily practice) and with the concept of **deliberate practice** (Anders Ericsson).
