# The Black Swan — Taleb

Nassim Nicholas Taleb's *The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable* (2007). "Want to read" — no personal notes yet in the Notion source. Bookmarked during the January 2022 reading-list build.

## Thesis (standard framing)

A "Black Swan" event has three properties:

1. **Outlier** — lies outside the realm of regular expectation because nothing in the past can convincingly point to its possibility.
2. **Extreme impact** — economic, historical, scientific.
3. **Retrospective predictability** — after the fact humans concoct explanations that make it appear less random and more predictable than it was.

Taleb's central argument: most of the world's variance (in finance, history, technology, fame) lives in Extremistan — fat-tailed distributions where a single event swamps the entire sample — yet we analyse it with tools built for Mediocristan (the thin-tailed Gaussian world).

## Concepts to expect on a read

- **The Ludic Fallacy** — mistaking the neat randomness of games for the messy randomness of life.
- **The Narrative Fallacy** — our compulsion to turn sequences of facts into explanatory stories, making the past look inevitable.
- **The Turkey Problem** — a turkey fed every day for 1,000 days has increasing confidence in farmers' benevolence, right up to Thanksgiving.
- **Epistemic arrogance** — the gap between what one thinks one knows and what one actually knows.
- **Silent evidence** — the graveyard of failed equivalent attempts that we never see.
- **Mediocristan vs. Extremistan** — two probabilistic worlds with different rules.
