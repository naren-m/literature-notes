# Cisco Interviewer Training — Behavioral Interviewing

Notes from an internal training on how to run structured behavioral interviews. The core claim: past performance predicts future performance — so dig for concrete, specific examples rather than generalities.

## The problem with traditional interviewing

The goal of an interview is to **predict success on the job**. Most interviews don't do this well because they rely on unstructured questions and gut feel.

Four principles of better interviews:

- Get clarity on hiring criteria before the interview.
- Go deep — get *evidence* of performance, not summaries.
- Look beyond traditional signals (GPA, years of experience, resume polish).
- Hire the *person*, not the resume.

## Validity coefficients — the science

Selection techniques ranked by predictive power:

**Higher predictive power**:

- Simulated job experience / work samples.
- Behavior-based interviewing.
- Job tryout.

**Lower predictive power**:

- References.
- GPA.
- Years of experience.
- Unstructured interviews.
- Amount of education.

Recommended focus: **behavior-based interviews** and **structured situational interviews** — put candidates in situations that demonstrate the specific skills you need.

## Past performance predicts future performance

Good behavioral questions sound like:

- "Where and how did you…?"
- "Walk me through the time you…"
- "Tell me about a time when…"

Good questions come from *knowing the job well*. Start by listing your hiring criteria across four dimensions:

- **Skills & knowledge**
- **Attributes**
- **Achievements**
- **Motivation**

## Reverse engineering the questions

1. Start from the criteria you want evidence of.
2. Write a question that would force the candidate to demonstrate that criterion through a past example.
3. Dig into the **how** — the specific actions the candidate took, not just the outcome.

## Setting up the question

Favorite question templates:

- "Tell me about a time you handled conflict with a teammate."
- "What was the most challenging thing you worked on, and why did it become challenging?"
- "Walk me through a decision you made where you had incomplete information."

## What counts as good evidence

- **Good**: specific examples, concrete demonstrations, named people/projects, quantifiable outcomes.
- **Bad**: generalities, lists of responsibilities, gut-feel answers, deflecting with brain teasers.

Observe both *what* the candidate answers and *how* they think about the question.

## Interview methodology — two primary axes

1. What they have done in the past (behavioral).
2. How they are performing in the current interview session (situational / real-time).

## Basic formula

Target a specific past behavior → probe how they did it → extract evidence of the underlying skill → judge whether that skill maps to the role.

## Go deep, get specific, get examples

"Great interviewers probe for evidence. They work hard to understand not just the **why** and **what**, but the **how**, too."

Probe questions: *How? What? Where? Why?*

## Combination questions

A single well-designed question can surface multiple dimensions at once — a conflict-resolution question reveals **skill** (communication), **attributes** (empathy, patience), **achievement** (the resolution), and **motivation** (what they were trying to protect).

## Using behavioral questions to assess knowledge

You can probe *knowledge* behaviorally too — not "Do you know X?" but "Tell me about the last time you used X to solve a real problem. What went wrong, and how did you fix it?" This filters out candidates who've memorized concepts without applying them.

## Situational techniques

For situations the candidate hasn't encountered, hypothetical situational questions can substitute — but only as a fallback. Real past behavior beats speculated future behavior.

## Open threads

- Draft a personal interview rubric per role type (IC vs. manager, junior vs. senior).
- Build a question bank tied to each of the 4 dimensions (skills / attributes / achievements / motivation).
