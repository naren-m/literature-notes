# The Ride of a Lifetime

Bob Iger's memoir of running ABC/Disney and what he learned about leadership along the way. Readwise-synced highlights — 46 at last count, all anchored to Kindle locations. Core lens: treat bad news as a solvable problem you have control over rather than something happening to you.

## Iger's leadership qualities

- **Optimism.** Pragmatic enthusiasm for what can be achieved. People are not motivated by pessimists.
- **Courage.** Risk-taking is essential in disrupted businesses; fear of failure destroys creativity.
- **Focus.** Allocate time, energy, and resources to what's highest value, and communicate priorities clearly and often.
- **Decisiveness.** Chronic indecision is corrosive to morale. Encourage diverse opinion but decide.
- **Curiosity.** The path to innovation begins here.
- **Fairness.** Empathy and accessibility. A culture of fear kills innovation.
- **Thoughtfulness.** Take the time to develop informed opinions.
- **Authenticity.** Truth and authenticity breed respect.
- **Relentless pursuit of perfection.** Not perfectionism at all costs — a refusal to accept mediocrity.
- **Integrity.** The way you do anything is the way you do everything.

## Roone Arledge and *shokunin*

Roone wasn't a yeller but was tough and exacting. His mantra: *"Do what you need to do to make it better."* Iger calls this the single thing that shaped him most. He later came to the Japanese word **shokunin** — "the endless pursuit of perfection for some greater good" — via the documentary *Jiro Dreams of Sushi*. The idea isn't perfectionism; it's refusing to accept mediocrity, pushing back on "good enough."

## On authority, humility, and being new

You can't fake what you don't know. First rule: don't pretend. Be humble — but don't let humility prevent you from leading. True authority and true leadership come from knowing who you are and not pretending otherwise.

## Dan Burke's trombone oil note

> Avoid getting into the business of manufacturing trombone oil. You may become the greatest trombone-oil manufacturer in the world, but in the end, the world only consumes a few quarts of trombone oil a year!

A note on not investing in projects that sap resources without giving much back. Iger credits it as one of the most useful leadership lessons he received.

## Priorities, road maps, and lowering anxiety

A CEO must provide a road map: *this is where we want to be; this is how we'll get there.* When priorities are articulated clearly, many downstream decisions become easier and organizational anxiety drops. If leaders don't articulate priorities clearly, the people around them don't know what their own should be.

## Empathy with excellence

- *Excellence and fairness don't have to be mutually exclusive.*
- Treat people with fairness and empathy, while keeping expectations high. Give second chances for honest mistakes.
- Empathy is a prerequisite to the sound management of creativity, and respect is critical.
- When giving notes, be mindful of how much of themselves the person has poured in.
- Don't start negatively and don't start small — starting petty makes you look petty.

## On succession

The dynamics between a CEO and the next-in-line are often fraught because we all want to believe we're irreplaceable. Good leadership isn't about being indispensable — it's about helping others prepare to step into your shoes. Give them access to your decision-making; be honest about the skills they still need.

## On firing

Iger's opener: *"I've asked you to come in here for a difficult reason."* Be direct about what wasn't working and why it isn't going to change. Acknowledge the decision was hard to make, and much harder on them.

## On hard times

It's easy to be optimistic when everyone tells you you're great. It's much harder — and much more necessary — when your sense of yourself is being challenged publicly. Iger returns to Theodore Roosevelt's *"The Man in the Arena"* speech: credit belongs to the one whose face is marred by dust and sweat and blood, not to the critic.

## Lucas and Star Wars

Negotiations with George Lucas over money, and over his ongoing creative involvement, were exercises in balancing respect for what Lucas had built and how personal it was for him with Iger's responsibility to Disney. Empathy, but not at the cost of clarity.

## Scott's insurgent framing

> You must think, plan, and act like an insurgent.

Iger's note against this was that his own overall vision lacked clarity and inspiration — and that the fix was the clear-priorities road map above.

## Final distillation

> To tell great stories, you need great talent. Now more than ever: innovate or die. There can be no innovation if you operate out of fear of the new.

Other distilled lessons: take responsibility when you screw up, value ability more than experience, put people in roles that require more of them than they know they have, ask the questions you need to ask, admit without apology what you don't understand. Managing creativity is an art, not a science.
