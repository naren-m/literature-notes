# Atomic Habits

Sticking to existing habits is easy — they run on autopilot. To make a habit stick, eliminate friction to start; to break one, introduce friction. Focus on who you want to become rather than the activity itself — identity is the stable handle for change.

## Three levels of change

1. Outcome change — what you get.
2. Process change — what you do.
3. Identity change — who you are.

The most effective lever is identity. Your identity emerges out of your habits; every action is a vote for the type of person you wish to become. Real habit work is editing beliefs about yourself and letting behavior follow.

> You do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems.

## The habit loop

A habit is a behavior repeated enough times to become automatic. Any habit breaks down into a feedback loop with four steps: **cue → craving → response → reward**. The cue is about noticing; craving is about wanting; response is about obtaining; reward is what satisfies. Split into two phases: *problem phase* (cue, craving) and *solution phase* (response, reward).

If any of the four steps fails, the habit does not form. Eliminate the cue → habit never starts. Reduce the craving → no motivation. Make the response difficult → you won't act. Break the reward → no reason to repeat.

## Four Laws of Behavior Change

To *build* a habit, invert the laws to *break* one:

- **1st (Cue):** Make it obvious → Make it invisible.
- **2nd (Craving):** Make it attractive → Make it unattractive.
- **3rd (Response):** Make it easy → Make it difficult.
- **4th (Reward):** Make it satisfying → Make it unsatisfying.

Ask four questions of any behavior you want to change: How can I make it obvious? attractive? easy? satisfying?

## Implementation intentions & habit stacking

An implementation intention is a plan made beforehand: "I will [BEHAVIOR] at [TIME] in [LOCATION]." People who write these are far more likely to follow through — the cues of time and location do the remembering for you.

Habit stacking is a specialized form: "After [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]." Works best when the cue is specific and immediately actionable. Anchor new behaviors to existing automatic ones.

## The 1% principle and the Plateau of Latent Potential

Dave Brailsford's "aggregation of marginal gains" — 1% improvement on every component compounds dramatically. Time magnifies the margin between success and failure. *Good habits make time your ally; bad habits make it your enemy.*

Change is tectonic: two plates grind for millions of years before the earthquake. The hundred-and-first hammer blow splits the stone, but all the earlier blows did the work. Progress looks invisible until it doesn't.

## Goals vs. systems

- Winners and losers have the same goals; systems distinguish them.
- Achieving a goal is a momentary state change.
- Goals restrict happiness to a future checkpoint — fix the inputs and outputs follow.
- Goals are at odds with long-term progress; the point is to keep playing.

## Awareness & identity moves

- Pointing-and-Calling (the Japanese rail-safety technique) raises unconscious habits into conscious ones by verbalizing; reduces errors up to 85%.
- Ask of any behavior: "Does this habit cast a vote for or against my desired identity?"
- Identity is *repeated being-ness* — the Latin *essentitas* (being) + *identidem* (repeatedly).
- Identity-based habits: decide who you want to be, then prove it with small wins.

## Akrasia & commitment devices

*Akrasia* — acting against your better judgement. Named by Aristotle and Socrates. Victor Hugo locked himself in his study by sending his clothes away, finishing his book two weeks early. A *commitment device* is a present choice that constrains your future self.

## Other notes

- People think they lack motivation when they lack clarity.
- Behavior is a function of the Person in their Environment: B = f(P, E).
- Diderot Effect: a new possession triggers a spiral of more consumption.
- Suggestion Impulse Buying (Hawkins Stern, 1952): seeing a product for the first time visualizes a need.
- Schiphol urinal flies cut bathroom-cleaning costs 8% per year — tiny cue, large behavioral shift.
- "Cue, routine, reward" from *The Power of Habit* maps onto B. F. Skinner's "stimulus, response, reward" (1930s operant conditioning).

> Until you make the unconscious conscious, it will direct your life and you will call it fate. — Carl Jung
