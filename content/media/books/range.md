# Range — Why Generalists Triumph in a Specialized World

David Epstein's *Range* (2019). The #1 NYT bestseller that pushed back against the "10,000 hours" / early-specialisation orthodoxy.

## Central argument

Anders Ericsson's **deliberate practice** school says: start early, focus narrowly, rack up the hours. Epstein looks at the data on top performers — athletes, scientists, Nobel laureates, inventors, forecasters — and finds:

> In most fields — especially those that are **complex and unpredictable** — generalists, not specialists, are primed to excel.

## Key contrasts

- **Tiger vs. Roger**: Tiger Woods was groomed into golf from age 2. Roger Federer tried skiing, wrestling, swimming, skateboarding, basketball, handball and many more sports before settling on tennis in his teens. Both ended up world #1.
- **Chess & golf** (kind learning environments, stable rules) reward early specialists.
- **Medicine, research, business, innovation** (wicked learning environments, shifting rules) reward generalists who can transfer patterns across domains.

## Provocative claims (publisher blurb)

- "Failing a test is the best way to learn." — desirable difficulties; testing effect.
- "Frequent quitters end up with the most fulfilling careers." — *match quality* improves with exploration.
- "The most impactful inventors cross domains rather than deepening their knowledge in a single area." — T-shaped > I-shaped.

## Why this matters in the age of AI

As narrow specialisation gets automated, **people who think broadly and embrace diverse experiences** gain leverage. The generalist advantage compounds.
