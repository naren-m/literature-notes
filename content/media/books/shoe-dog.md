# Shoe Dog — Phil Knight

Phil Knight's *Shoe Dog: A Memoir by the Creator of Nike* (2016). 43 Readwise highlights, 4/5, finished 2022-02-12. Knight tells the story of Nike's founding as a Crazy Idea financed by $50, a Stanford business paper, and an import arrangement with Onitsuka Tiger.

## The opening instruction to himself (1962)

> Let everyone else call your idea crazy… just keep going. Don't stop. Don't even think about stopping until you get there, and don't give much thought to where "there" is. Whatever comes, just don't stop. *(Location 86)*

The refrain of the whole book. Combined with the Lewis Carroll quote — "it takes all the running you can do, to keep in the same place" — it frames Nike's early years as perpetual, desperate acceleration to avoid dissolution.

## Zen thread

Knight folds his reading of Zen (via D. T. Suzuki and *Zen in the Art of Archery*) into the business narrative:

> I was a linear thinker, and according to Zen linear thinking is nothing but a delusion… Reality is nonlinear, Zen says. No future, no past. All is now. *(Location 300)*

> Victory, Zen says, comes when we forget the self and the opponent, who are but two halves of one whole. *(Location 305)*

> You cannot travel the path until you have become the path yourself, said the Buddha. *(Location 320)*

The Japanese aesthetic — "Expect nothing, seek nothing, grasp nothing" — becomes a counterweight to the brute-force striving the business demanded.

## Management koan he repeats three times

> Don't tell people how to do things, tell them what to do and let them surprise you with their results. *(Locations 489, 1257, 4142)*

Attributed to General Douglas MacArthur; Knight quotes it three times across the book. This is the management principle that let Blue Ribbon / Nike scale without Knight personally owning every decision.

## "Fail fast"

> I wasn't much for setting goals, but this goal kept flashing through my mind every day, until it became my internal chant: Fail fast. *(Location 1311)*

Predates the Silicon Valley cliché by decades. Knight was operating at the edge of bankruptcy for most of the 1970s; "fail fast" for him was literal — find out whether the shipment was going to survive customs, fast.

## Belief sells product

> I believed in running. I believed that if people got out and ran a few miles every day, the world would be a better place, and I believed these shoes were better to run in. People, sensing my belief, wanted some of that belief for themselves. Belief, I decided. Belief is irresistible. *(Location 768)*

The whole book's answer to "what was the edge?" The product was commoditised; the conviction wasn't.

## On the accounting primitive

> Assets equal liabilities plus equity. This foundational equation, I said, must always, always be in balance. *(Location 1675)*

> Again and again I learned that lack of equity was a leading cause of failure. *(Location 1127)*

The sub-plot of the book is a cash-flow crisis: banks pulling credit, sales outrunning the balance sheet. The dual-class-stock device (Location 4734) was invented to solve this without losing control.

## Prefontaine — the emotional centre

Steve Prefontaine, Oregon miler coached by Bill Bowerman (Knight's co-founder), is the spiritual mascot:

> Somebody may beat me—but they're going to have to bleed to do it. *(Location 3802)*

Bowerman's own framing, used for both running and company-building:

> The cowards never started and the weak died along the way—that leaves us. *(Location 2970, repeated 4857)*

## On why business

> But for us business was no more about making money than being human is about making blood. Yes, the human body needs blood. *(Location 4822)*

> When you make something, when you improve something, when you deliver something… making them happier, or healthier, or safer, or better… you're participating more fully in the whole grand human drama. More than simply alive, you're helping others to live more fully, and if that's business, all right, call me a businessman. *(Location 4828)*

The closing statement of purpose. Worth pairing with the opening "Crazy Idea" — the book arcs from a kid's imagined business to a philosophy of creation.

## Takeaways for the vault

- **"Fail fast" — 1960s version.** Predates the startup-culture slogan.
- **The MacArthur delegation rule** — a concrete hiring/management principle.
- **Belief > product.** When the product is commoditised, conviction is the edge.
- **Equity-starvation is the #1 cause of business death.** Revenue growth without capital will strangle a company.
- **Dual-class stock as a founder-preservation mechanism.** Knight pulled this off in the 1980s.
- **Prefontaine bleed-for-it quote** — reusable anchor for any personal-effort writing.
