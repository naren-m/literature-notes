# 1% Better Every Day — James Clear

James Clear's talk ("The Entrepreneur's Organization" talk, ~50 minutes on YouTube). Rated 5/5 — finished 2020-07-13. This is effectively the spoken-form preview of [[atomic-habits]].

## Gist

- Small improvements, tiny habits → drastic changes over time (compound interest on behaviour).
- Good habits make **time your ally**; bad habits make time your enemy.
- Many people think they lack **motivation**; what they lack is **clarity**.
- True change is **identity change**:
  - The goal is not to read a book, the goal is to become a reader.
  - The goal is not to write a program, the goal is to become an engineer.

## The four stages of habits

Clear walks the talk through the four stages (cue / craving / response / reward → "noticing / wanting / doing / enjoying").

### 1. Noticing — implementation intentions

Study with three groups tracked for exercise adherence:

1. **Track your exercise** only.
2. Tracking **plus motivation** (video of how exercise reduces heart disease).
3. Tracking, motivation, **plus a clear implementation intention**: *"During the next week, I will partake in at least 20 minutes of vigorous exercise on [DAY] at [TIME] at/in [PLACE]."*

Result: group 3 was **2×–3× more likely to follow through**. A concrete plan removes decision-making at the point of action.

> Give your goals a time and a place to live in the world. Give it some space in the calendar.

### Failure premortem

- Fast-forward six months. Imagine you **have failed** to achieve your most important goal. Write the story of how it happened.
- Corporate version: the "kill-the-company exercise."
- Surfacing the failure paths before they happen makes them avoidable.

### 2. Wanting — environment design

> One of the most overlooked drivers of habits is your physical environment.

- Change behaviour *without talking to people* — change the environment.
- Keep the guitar visible; don't rely on willpower.
- **More steps between you and bad habits; fewer steps between you and good habits.**

### 3. Doing — reps

Photography experiment: two groups:
- **Quality group** — produce *one perfect photograph*.
- **Quantity group** — produce *many photographs*.

The best photographs came from the **quantity group** — they iterated, made mistakes, and improved. Quality follows quantity.

- In the beginning just put the reps in.
- Any outcome is a point on the spectrum of repetition.
- Make it easy to *start*. Any habit can be started in **two minutes**.
- Optimise for the **starting line**, not the finish line. Meditation can start as two minutes sitting quietly. Once friction at the start is zero, duration takes care of itself.

### 4. Enjoying — short-term rewards for good habits

- Good habits have *immediate costs* and *delayed rewards* — the opposite of what the brain likes.
- You need a mechanism to front-load enjoyment: the **Seinfeld strategy** ("Don't break the chain") + **Never miss twice**.
- Introduces gamification into the reward cycle.

## Takeaways for the vault

- The **Implementation Intention sentence** — literal template to use in any habit plan.
- The **Two-Minute Rule** — any habit must be reducible to a 2-minute entry.
- **Identity > outcomes** — what matters is "who am I becoming?" not "what do I want?"
- **Environment beats willpower.**
- **Quantity beats quality in the learning phase.**
