# Wormholes

Research stub on wormholes — hypothetical tunnels through spacetime connecting two distant points (or even two different universes).

*(Title note: the original Notion page was titled "Warm Holes" — a typo for "wormholes" preserved here via cross-reference but corrected in the note title.)*

## The idea

A **wormhole** is a solution to Einstein's field equations that connects two regions of spacetime via a "throat." Traversing the throat could, in principle:

1. Shortcut from one point in space to another faster than going through intervening space.
2. Move an observer forward or backward in time, depending on how the mouths age relative to each other.

The original notes captured this intuition:

> Moving in one direction through a wormhole moves time in one direction; reversing moves time in the other. Traveling backward in time would spawn a new branching timeline — the "Flashverse" from *The Flash* comics / show.

*(Whether branching timelines are real physics or just a convenient fiction is an open question — see the chronology protection conjecture.)*

## Types of wormholes

- **Schwarzschild wormhole (Einstein-Rosen bridge)** — mathematically appears in the maximally extended Schwarzschild solution, but is **non-traversable**: the throat closes faster than light could cross it.
- **Traversable wormhole (Morris-Thorne, 1988)** — requires **exotic matter** with negative energy density to hold the throat open. Violates the null energy condition.
- **Lorentzian vs. Euclidean** — Lorentzian wormholes are "in" ordinary spacetime; Euclidean wormholes are mathematical constructs used in quantum gravity path integrals (ER=EPR conjecture).

## The exotic matter problem

Keeping a wormhole's throat open against gravitational collapse requires matter that produces **repulsive gravity** — technically, matter violating the averaged null energy condition (ANEC). In classical physics, no known matter does this. Quantum field theory permits small violations (Casimir effect, squeezed vacuum states), but it's unclear whether enough violation can be arranged in the right geometry.

## Time travel via wormholes (Morris, Thorne & Yurtsever 1988)

If one mouth of a wormhole is moved at relativistic speed (or parked near a black hole) while the other stays put, time dilation causes them to age differently. An observer entering the younger mouth exits from the older mouth — effectively traveling back in time. This is the canonical "time machine."
