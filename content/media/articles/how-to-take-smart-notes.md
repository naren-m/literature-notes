# How to Take Smart Notes (Nat Eliason, on Ahrens's book)

Nat Eliason's essay walking through his adaptation of Sönke Ahrens's *How to Take Smart Notes* — itself the best English-language presentation of **Niklas Luhmann's Zettelkasten** method. Luhmann, a 20th-century sociologist, published 70 books and 400+ articles; he described the work as *effortless* because of his note-taking system.

## The core insight

> *…the mere copying of quotes almost always changes their meaning by stripping them out of context, even though the words aren't changed. This is a common beginner mistake, which can only lead to a patchwork of ideas, but never a coherent thought.* — Ahrens, p. 75

Highlighting is a waste of time if you don't capture the *idea the highlight gave you*. A highlight speaks at the moment you mark it; a week later, re-reading it, you often can't reconstruct why. The Zettelkasten system says: **write the idea down in your own words while it's fresh.**

## The five-step adaptation

### 1. Take physical notes as you read

Hand-write, not type. "Handwriting makes pure copying impossible." (p. 78) Eliason uses a large Moleskine + a pocket Field Notes. Capture **fleeting notes** — your own interpretation, or some tangential idea the text sparked — with the page number.

### 2. Record bibliographical sources

Eliason deviates slightly from Ahrens here: he also tabs the passages that prompted an idea, so he can pull direct quotes for later articles. **Only tab a passage if it inspired you to write something down.** Don't tab things that "seem important."

### 3. Upload your notes

Two kinds:

- **References** — the original highlights. Eliason uses [Readwise](https://readwise.io/) to scan/export them.
- **Ideas** — the thoughts that the book sparked. Re-type from the notebook and expand each into a **fully-formed standalone thought**.

The key question for each idea: *how can I make this detailed enough to stand on its own, without the book or the associated highlight?*

### 4. File your notes — by future context, not source

> *In the old system, the question is: Under which topic do I store this note? In the new system, the question is: In which context will I want to stumble on it again?* — Ahrens, p. 40

An idea about urgency, sparked by *Let My People Go Surfing*, should not live under "Let My People Go Surfing." It should live under "Urgency" or "Persuasion" — wherever future-you is most likely to need it. Tag with source for provenance, file by *future utility*.

### 5. Use and organize

Once several notes have collected under a context, they become the skeleton of articles, essays, or deliverables. Eliason: "Even a few ideas from a couple books dropped into a topic form a jumping off point for a bunch of other ideas, and make it easier to get past writer's block."

## Why this matters for the vault

This essay is effectively the **manifesto for this entire literature-notes vault**. The Foam / Obsidian / Roam markdown model exists to operationalise exactly this pattern:

- Literature notes under `content/media/` = References.
- Research notes under `content/research/` = Ideas.
- `[[wikilinks]]` are the "file by future context" mechanism — an idea is discoverable from any context that links to it.
- `content/journal/` captures fleeting notes before they've earned a home.
