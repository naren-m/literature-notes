# Amarakosha — Chapter 2 (Dvitīya-kāṇḍa / Bhū-kāṇḍa)

The second *kāṇḍa* of the Amarakosha covers the **earthly and human-social** vocabulary of Sanskrit: earth and geography, towns, mountains, forests, plants, wild animals, humans, the four varṇas (social orders), and the specialised vocabulary each of them brings (brahmin ritual, kṣatriya kingship and warfare, vaiśya trade and agriculture, śūdra crafts).

## Ten vargas of the Bhū-kāṇḍa

1. **Bhūvarga** (भूवर्गः) — Earth, soil types, ground, land divisions, continents (dvīpas), mountains in the abstract.
2. **Puravarga** (पुरवर्गः) — Towns, cities, villages, roads, houses, palaces, gates, walls, streets, parts of buildings, furniture.
3. **Shailavarga** (शैलवर्गः) — Specific mountains (Meru, Himavat, Vindhya, Malaya, etc.), peaks, caves, ridges, metals, gems, minerals from mountains.
4. **Vanaushadhivarga** (वनौषधिवर्गः) — Forests, trees, flowers, medicinal herbs, roots, fruits, bark, leaves, woods. A vast section — one of the largest in the whole Amarakosha.
5. **Simhadivarga** (सिंहादिवर्गः) — Wild animals: lions, tigers, elephants, deer, monkeys, boars, bears, jackals, wolves. Includes hunting vocabulary and animal body parts.
6. **Manushyavarga** (मनुष्यवर्गः) — Humans generically: body parts (anatomy), ages, sexes, kinship terms, emotions, character types.
7. **Brāhmavarga** (ब्राह्मवर्गः) — Brahmin-class vocabulary: Vedic rituals, yajñas, offerings, sacred fires, sacrificial implements, mantras, ritualists' roles.
8. **Kshatriyavarga** (क्षत्रियवर्गः) — Kṣatriya (warrior/ruler) class vocabulary: kings, ministers, armies, weapons, chariots, war, strategy, diplomacy.
9. **Vaishyavarga** (वैश्यवर्गः) — Vaiśya (merchant/farmer) class vocabulary: agriculture, grains, cattle, trade, money, measures, markets.
10. **Shudravarga** (शूद्रवर्गः) — Śūdra-class vocabulary: crafts, tools, artisans, occupations, servants.

## Scholarly note

The Bhū-kāṇḍa's **Vanauṣadhivarga** is historically the most cited portion of the Amarakosha outside India-specific contexts: it preserves a huge synonym list for medicinal plants and trees and has been used for centuries by Ayurvedic practitioners to identify species in old manuscripts. The **Manuṣyavarga** is similarly foundational for Sanskrit anatomical vocabulary — most classical medical texts (Caraka, Suśruta) take this terminology as baseline.
