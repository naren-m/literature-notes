# No Rules Rules — Netflix and the Culture of Reinvention

Reed Hastings (Netflix co-founder) with Erin Meyer (author of *The Culture Map*). Status: **In Progress**. FT/McKinsey Business Book of the Year shortlist (2020).

## Central argument

Netflix's ability to reinvent itself — from DVD-by-mail to streaming to original content — is impossible with conventional command-and-control management. The operating system is **freedom and responsibility**, expressed through a handful of counter-intuitive policies.

## The Netflix principles (as framed in the publisher's blurb)

- **No vacation or expense policies.** Adults manage their own time and spend.
- **Adequate performance gets a generous severance.** Only top performers stay. The "Keeper Test" — *would I fight to keep this person?*
- **Hard work is irrelevant.** Output, not effort.
- **Don't try to please your boss — give candid feedback instead.** Radical transparency, 360° reviews, stop-start-continue.
- **Employees don't need approval.** Context, not controls.
- **Pay top of market.** One person at the top of the market outperforms two mid-market hires.

## Three-stage ramp (Hastings's framing)

1. Build up **talent density** — only A-players.
2. Increase **candor** — normalize truth-telling up and down the hierarchy.
3. Remove **controls** — policies, approvals, limits fall away as 1 and 2 mature.

Each stage unlocks the next. Going out of order produces chaos.

## Erin Meyer's contribution

Meyer (INSEAD) adds the **cross-cultural** lens: Netflix's candor norms land very differently in the Netherlands, Japan, Brazil, India. The book systematically addresses how the principles need translation rather than transplantation.
