# Niruktam — Yāska's Nirukta

Migration of a Notion standalone page that held a bookmark to the **Nirukta** (निरुक्तम्) — the oldest surviving Sanskrit etymological treatise, attributed to **Yāska** (~6th c. BCE, pre-Panini). The Nirukta is one of the six **Vedāṅgas** (limbs of the Veda), specifically the vedāṅga of *etymology*; the other five are Śikṣā (phonetics), Chandas (metre), Vyākaraṇa (grammar), Kalpa (ritual), and Jyotiṣa (astrology/astronomy).

## About Yāska's Nirukta

Yāska's Nirukta is the oldest extant work on Sanskrit etymology (*nirukta* literally means "interpreted" or "explained"). Key features:

- **Commentary on the Nighaṇṭu** — The Nirukta is a systematic commentary on an older list of Vedic word-lists called the *Nighaṇṭu*; Yāska organises them into chapters (*adhyāyas*) and explains each word.
- **Twelve chapters** — Divided into twelve *adhyāyas*, further grouped into three *kāṇḍas*: Naighaṇṭuka (synonyms of common concepts), Naigama (obscure/rare Vedic words), and Daivata (names of deities).
- **Pre-Paninian grammatical theory** — Yāska predates Pāṇini by a few centuries and preserves grammatical doctrines in a state before Pāṇini's systematisation. The famous "dispute with Śākaṭāyana" over whether all nouns derive from verbal roots is in the Nirukta — a question that modern morphological theory still finds non-trivial.
- **Bhāṣā method** — Yāska's method is to derive Sanskrit words from verbal roots (*dhātus*) and explain their meaning based on that derivation. This is the canonical statement of what became the *dhātu-pāṭha* tradition.

## Why the Nirukta matters for Sanskrit study

- It is the **starting text** for anyone who wants to read Vedic Sanskrit (Rig-Veda etc.) rather than Classical Sanskrit — the Nirukta explains the unusual Vedic vocabulary that later reference works (Amarakosha, Medinikosha) don't cover.
- It preserves **ancient grammatical debates** that Pāṇini later resolved (or suppressed): in the Nirukta we see Śākaṭāyana's "everything from roots" position arguing with the conservative "some nouns are primitive" position.
- It is the **template for the word-analysis style** of the Dharmaśāstra and Nyāya traditions — medieval commentators routinely justify their readings by citing Yāska's derivations.
