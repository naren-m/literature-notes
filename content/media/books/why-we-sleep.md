# Why We Sleep

Matthew Walker's *Why We Sleep: Unlocking the Power of Sleep and Dreams* (2017). Rated 5/5. Notes are a curated digest of the Readwise-synced highlights the Notion page collected (several hundred in total) — reduced here to the most memorable claims and ideas, organised by the book's four parts.

## Headline claims

- Sleep is "the single most effective thing we can do to reset our brain and body health each day — Mother Nature's best effort yet at contra-death."
- Sleeping less than 6–7 hours a night demolishes the immune system and more than doubles the risk of cancer.
- The shorter your sleep, the shorter your life span. "I'll sleep when I'm dead" is bad advice.

## Part 1: This Thing Called Sleep

### Chapter 1 — To Sleep

- Can you recall the last time you woke up without an alarm, feeling refreshed, not needing caffeine?
- Dreaming provides a consoling neurochemical bath that mollifies painful memories, and a virtual-reality space in which the brain melds past and present knowledge, inspiring creativity.

### Chapter 2 — Caffeine, Jet Lag, Melatonin

- Everyone has a **circadian rhythm** (*circa* = around, *dian* = day). The master clock is the **suprachiasmatic nucleus** (SCN), sitting above the optic chiasm (*supra* + *chiasm*).
- A signal used to reset the clock is called a **zeitgeber** ("time giver").
- Not everyone's circadian timing is the same — larks and owls are real.
- **Melatonin** is the *timing* signal, not the sleep itself. Walker's metaphor: melatonin is the Olympic starter who says "runners, on your mark" and fires the pistol — it tells the body *when* to sleep but does not participate in the race of sleep.
- After a time-zone change, the SCN can only readjust by about **one hour per day**.

### Chapter 3 — Defining Sleep

- Sleep is defined behaviourally (reduced responsiveness, stereotyped posture) and electrically (EEG signatures).

### Chapter 4 — Who Sleeps and How

- Unihemispheric sleep in dolphins and many birds: half a brain sleeps at a time.

### Chapter 5 — Sleep Across the Lifespan

- REM dominates infant sleep and is essential for neural wiring.
- Teenage circadian phase-delay is biological, not laziness — school start times are fighting adolescent biology.
- With age, sleep becomes fragmented and less deep — a driver of cognitive decline.

## Part 2: Why Should You Sleep?

### Chapter 6 — Benefits for the Brain

- Sleep before learning prepares the brain to *make* memories; sleep after learning consolidates them.
- Naps in the afternoon restore the capacity to learn.

### Chapter 7 — Sleep Deprivation and the Brain

- After 19–20 hours awake, mental function is impaired equivalent to being legally drunk.
- The prefrontal cortex loses control over the amygdala: emotional volatility, a "reptilian" threat response.
- Drowsy driving kills more people than drunk and drugged driving combined.

### Chapter 8 — Sleep Deprivation and the Body

- Short sleep correlates with: heart attacks, strokes, hypertension, type-2 diabetes, obesity, depression, anxiety, and shortened telomeres.
- Leptin / ghrelin dysregulation: the sleep-deprived brain wants more food, especially carbs.
- The cell-phagocytic activity of natural-killer cells drops after a single night of 4-hour sleep.

## Part 3: How and Why We Dream

### Chapter 9 — Routinely Psychotic: REM-Sleep Dreaming

- In REM, you are: (1) hallucinating, (2) delusional, (3) disoriented in time/place/person, (4) emotionally volatile — the clinical definition of psychosis.
- The paralysis of REM (REM atonia) keeps the body from acting out the dream.

### Chapter 10 — Overnight Therapy

- REM sleep strips the visceral emotional charge from memories while preserving their informational content. Without REM, trauma stays raw.

### Chapter 11 — Dream Creativity

- Problems left unsolved before sleep are frequently solved through dream-state recombination of knowledge (Mendeleev, Kekulé, Dmitri).
- Lucid dreaming is real but rare; evidence from wrist/clenched-fist signals inside REM.

## Part 4: From Sleeping Pills to Society Transformed

### Chapter 12 — Sleep Disorders

- Insomnia, narcolepsy, sleep apnoea, fatal familial insomnia.

### Chapter 13 — What's Stopping You From Sleeping

- **Blue light, late-evening screens**: LED/iPad use before bed suppresses melatonin and shifts sleep onset.
- Alcohol is a sedative, not a sleep aid — it fragments sleep and obliterates REM.
- Caffeine's half-life is ~5–7 hours; quarter-life ~10–14. The afternoon coffee lingers.

### Chapter 14 — Pills vs. Therapy

- Sleeping pills produce sedation, not sleep — and have been associated with increased mortality and cancer risk.
- **CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Insomnia)** is the evidence-based first-line treatment. Sleep restriction, stimulus control, sleep hygiene.

### Chapter 15 — What Society Is Doing Wrong (and Right)

- Medicine: sleep-deprived doctors make more errors; residency training schedules are dangerous.
- Education: adolescents need later school start times; districts that have moved start times later see grades, attendance and traffic-accident rates improve.
- Industry: NASA and Google treat sleep as performance-critical.

### Chapter 16 — A New Vision

- Treat sleep as a public-health pillar alongside diet and exercise. The world's forgotten lever for longevity, creativity, and compassion.

## Walker's 12 tips for healthy sleep

1. Stick to a sleep schedule (same time to bed, same time up — including weekends).
2. Exercise — but not within 2–3 hours of bed.
3. Avoid caffeine and nicotine.
4. Avoid alcohol before bed.
5. Avoid large meals / beverages late at night.
6. If possible, avoid medicines that delay or disrupt sleep.
7. No naps after 3 p.m.
8. Relax before bed — bath, reading, not work.
9. Bedroom: dark, gadget-free, cool (~65 °F / 18 °C).
10. Expose yourself to daylight in the morning.
11. Don't lie in bed awake — get up and do something calming.
12. Don't go to bed unless sleepy.

## Takeaways for the vault

- **The biological case for 8 hours** — quantified, not rhetorical.
- **Sleep = memory consolidation + emotional processing** — what makes REM non-negotiable for learning-heavy life.
- **Caffeine / alcohol / screens form the modern anti-sleep trident** — the ways we're unwittingly wrecking our own nights.
- **CBT-I, not pills** — the evidence-based insomnia treatment.
- **Later school start times** — the public-policy no-brainer.
