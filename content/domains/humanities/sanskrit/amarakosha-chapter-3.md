# Amarakosha — Chapter 3 (Tṛtīya-kāṇḍa / Sāmānya-kāṇḍa)

The third and shortest *kāṇḍa* of the Amarakosha is the **miscellany chapter**. Where Chapters 1 and 2 catalogued vocabulary by semantic field (gods, time, earth, humans, etc.), Chapter 3 handles the grammatical and philological *leftovers* — adjectives that don't fit any semantic category, homonyms (polysemous words), indeclinables, and a compact "gender and number" reference at the end.

## Five sections of the Sāmānya-kāṇḍa

1. **Viśeṣya-nighna-varga** (विशेष्यनिघ्नवर्गः) — Adjectives and qualifiers grouped by what they qualify. Covers colour, size, quality, character traits that attach to nouns across semantic categories (e.g. "tall" can apply to a tree or a person).
2. **Saṃkīrṇa-varga** (सङ्कीर्णवर्गः) — The "mixed" section: miscellaneous words that don't fall under any other varga. A catch-all for odd vocabulary.
3. **Nānārtha-varga** (नानार्थवर्गः) — **Homonyms**: words with multiple distinct meanings, organised by the word rather than by meaning. This is the famous section where students learn that *go* means cow, earth, heaven, Moon, eye, speech, thunderbolt, zodiac sign Taurus — depending on context.
4. **Avyaya-varga** (अव्ययवर्गः) — **Indeclinables**: particles, conjunctions, adverbs, interjections, prepositions, and the large Sanskrit class of *avyaya* words that don't inflect. Covers *api, ca, tu, hi, eva, kila, nu, yathā, tathā,* etc.
5. **Liṅgādi-saṅgraha-varga** (लिङ्गादिसङ्ग्रहवर्गः) — **Gender and grammatical summary**: compact rules for determining the gender, number, and declension class of nouns — the "appendix" that makes Amarakosha usable as a grammar reference.

## Why Chapter 3 matters

For practical Sanskrit reading, Chapter 3 is arguably the **most useful** of the three:

- The **Nānārtha-varga** is the one-stop reference when you encounter a polysemous word in a text and need to see all its possible meanings laid out in metrical form.
- The **Avyaya-varga** is a complete handbook of Sanskrit particles — these are notoriously hard to look up in modern dictionaries because they're listed by form and not by semantic field.
- The **Liṅgādi-saṅgraha** is how generations of Sanskrit students learned to assign the correct gender to unfamiliar nouns.

Chapters 1 and 2 are primarily synonym catalogues; Chapter 3 is a **working grammarian's reference**.
