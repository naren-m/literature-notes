# Introduction to Mathematical Thinking — Keith Devlin (Stanford / Coursera)

Keith Devlin's Coursera course. **Status: dropped** ("Not Interesting" in Notion). The instructor's framing — *"the course is about thinking, not about finding new techniques"* — did not land. But Week 1 produced real notes before the drop; they're preserved below.

### Introductory material

- Math uses language in a restricted way, to avoid confusions.
  - Example: "Object a has property p. 3 is a prime number."
  - Example: "If p is a prime of the form 4n + 1, then p is a sum of two squares."
- These precise statements can be joined with **combinators**.
  - Example: "3 is a prime OR 10 is not a prime."

**Gauss's theorem on primes of form 4n + 1.** If *p* is a prime of the form 4n + 1, then *p* is a sum of two squares — i.e. *p* is a Pythagorean number.

- Ancient Greek mathematicians noticed that all mathematical statements can be expressed in simple forms.
- **Forms of mathematical statement** — *and*, *or*, *not*, *implies*, *for all*, *there exists*. The Greeks analysed their behaviour and provided universally accepted meanings.
- When the study of the forms of math-in-English is carried out in a formal way, it is called **formal logic** or **mathematical logic**.

## Why the course was dropped

From the summary field: "Idea is about thinking not finding new techniques." Meaning: the course delivers on its title (mathematical *thinking*) rather than on computational tools. If the goal is to learn proof technique or discrete-math primitives, Devlin's later book *Introduction to Mathematical Thinking* (and the MIT 6.042 course [[mit-6042-math-for-cs]]) are more direct.
