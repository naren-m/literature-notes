# Surya Siddhanta

Stub on the **Sūrya Siddhānta** — a foundational Sanskrit astronomy treatise, one of the earliest systematic texts in Indian mathematical astronomy.

## What it is

- A **Sanskrit astronomical treatise** dated (in its surviving form) to roughly the 4th–5th century CE, though it claims much older origins.
- One of the **Siddhāntic texts** — alongside Brahmasphuṭasiddhānta (Brahmagupta), Pañca-siddhāntikā (Varāhamihira), and others — that collectively established pre-modern Indian astronomy.
- Structured as a verse treatise that a student would memorize and unpack with a commentary.

## What it covers

- **Planetary motion**: mean and true positions of Sun, Moon, and the five visible planets (Mercury through Saturn).
- **Eclipses**: prediction of solar and lunar eclipses via geometric methods, including corrections for parallax.
- **Trigonometry**: extensive use of **sine (jyā)** tables — the Indian sine function, an early ancestor of the modern sine.
- **Calendrical calculations**: yugas, tithis, nakṣatras, ayanāṃśa (precession).
- **Earth's dimensions and the celestial sphere**: spherical Earth (explicitly), circumference estimates, axial tilt.

## Notable technical claims

- **Heliocentric-compatible calculations** in places, though the text is formally geocentric.
- **Sidereal year** estimated extremely close to the modern value (off by a small fraction of a day).
- **Computation of planetary apsides** and **epicycles** bearing structural resemblance to Ptolemaic methods, arrived at independently.
- **Jyā and utkrama-jyā** tables — essentially sine and versine — with tabulated values at 3°45′ intervals (24 entries over a quadrant).

## Why it matters

- Represents **independent, sophisticated development** of mathematical astronomy outside the Greek tradition.
- Influenced later **Arabic astronomy** (via translation), which in turn fed back into Europe during the Islamic Golden Age.
- Still cited in **Hindu calendrical practice** (pañcāṅga) today for computing festival dates.
