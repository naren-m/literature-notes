# Planetary Linguistics — Latin, Greek, Sanskrit & Different Languages

Migration of a Notion Resources-DB row bookmarking **nineplanets.org's "Planetary Linguistics"** article — a cross-language catalogue of planet names (Latin, Greek, Sanskrit, Mandarin, Cantonese, Japanese, Korean, Thai, Arabic, Hebrew, plus dozens of European vernaculars and some indigenous and constructed languages). Kept in the Sanskrit cluster because the Sanskrit linguistic thread is the anchor the author was interested in.

## Scope of the original article

English is generally recognised as the international language for professional astronomy, but the various languages have their own vernacular planet names. The article tabulates them across many languages for the 10 classical bodies (Sun, Moon, and the 8 planets out to Pluto, which at the time of writing the article was still counted).

## Key observations

### Chinese / Japanese / Korean pattern

Mandarin and Cantonese names mean (approximately):
- Mercury — "Star of Water"
- Venus — "Star of Gold"
- Earth — "Sphere of Ground" (Earth)
- Moon — "Sphere of Moon"
- Mars — "Star of Fire"
- Jupiter — "Star of Wood"
- Saturn — "Star of Soil"
- Uranus — "Star of the King of Heaven"
- Neptune — "Star of the King of the Ocean"
- Pluto — "Star of the King of Hell"
- Sun — "The Brightest"

The five classical planets are named for the **Chinese five elements** (wood, fire, earth, metal, water) rather than gods.

Japanese and Korean use native words for "Moon" (*tsuki*, *dal*) but imported all other planetary names from Chinese — so the five-element pattern carried into Japanese and Korean scientific astronomy verbatim.

Uranus, Neptune, and Pluto (being post-classical discoveries) are just translations of the Western names across East Asian languages.

### Thai scientific vocabulary

Thai has many scientific and theological terms taken from Sanskrit, but their pronunciation has drifted considerably. A more phonetical presentation of Thai celestial bodies (from a correspondent cited in the article):

- Sun = (1) *suriya* (mythological); (2) *pra-atit* (high language); (3) *tawan* (colloquial)
- Mercury = [dau]-put
- Venus = [dau-pra]-suk
- Earth = *lok* (also "world")
- Moon = [pra]-jan
- Mars = [dau]-angkan
- Jupiter = [dau]-parihat-bodi
- Saturn = [dau]-sau
- Uranus = [dau]-marita-yu
- Neptune = [dau]-ket
- Pluto = no native term (English used)

*"Dau"* or *"dao"* literally means "star"; it is prefixed before a planet's name (because these names also double for days of the week — *dau-sau* = Saturn, *wan-sau* = Saturday, *wan* = day). *"Pra"* nominates a God (great, holy) — used for Sun and Moon because they are obviously not mere "dau" (stars).

### Seven-day week astrology

The seven-day system is based on the ancient astrological notion that the seven known celestial bodies influence what happens on Earth and that each controls the first hour of the day named for it. The system was brought into Hellenistic Egypt from Mesopotamia (where astrology had been practised for millennia and 7 was a propitious number). **In AD 321, Emperor Constantine the Great** grafted this astrological system onto the Roman calendar, made Sunday a day of rest and worship, and imposed the names and sequence still used in most of Western Europe.

In the **Germanic languages** (e.g. Old English), four of the Roman gods were converted into their Germanic equivalents:
- *dies Martis* → *Tīwesdæg* (Tuesday, Tīw = Mars)
- *dies Mercurii* → *Wōdnesdæg* (Wednesday, Woden = Mercury)
- *dies Iovis* → *Þunresdæg* (Thursday, Thor = Jupiter)
- *dies Veneris* → *Frīgedæg* (Friday, Frigg = Venus)

Latin names for weekdays survive in the modern Romance languages, though the weekend names were altered. Japanese weekdays also correspond indirectly to the same planets (via Chinese five-element names).

### Numbered days — the Semitic pattern

Prof. Neves of Universidade Nova de Lisboa pointed out: **at least in Hebrew, Greek, Arabic, and Portuguese** (and languages influenced by them, like Timorese Tetum), the days of the week are **numbered**. Sunday is number one (or "Day of the Lord" in Portuguese — *Domingo* — and in Greek — *Κυριακή / Kiriaki*). Friday is number six (except in Arabic, which calls it "Day of the Gathering"). All four languages use a word cognate with "Sabbath" (*Sabado*, *as-Sabt*) for Saturday.

### Adjective forms

English adjectives relating to planets do not always derive simply from the noun forms — the etymologies are Latin and Greek rather than English (e.g. *solar* from *sol*, *lunar* from *luna*, *jovial* from *Iovis*, *martial* from *Mars*).

### Month names

Two of the English month names have planetary connections: **January** is named for Janus; **March** is named for Mars.

## Sanskrit-linguistic thread

The article specifically notes **Sanskrit planet names** alongside the Romance, Germanic, Semitic, and East Asian ones. In Sanskrit:

| Body | Sanskrit | Root/Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sun | Sūrya (सूर्य), Āditya, Ravi | "shining"; "son of Aditi"; "possessing the luminary" |
| Moon | Candra (चन्द्र), Soma | "bright"; the soma plant / juice |
| Mercury | Budha (बुध) | "awakened, wise" |
| Venus | Śukra (शुक्र) | "bright, pure" |
| Mars | Maṅgala (मङ्गल) | "auspicious" |
| Jupiter | Bṛhaspati (बृहस्पति), Guru | "lord of the great" |
| Saturn | Śani (शनि) | from *śanaiḥ* "slowly" |
| Rāhu / Ketu | the lunar nodes, treated as "shadow planets" in jyotiṣa |

The **Sanskrit day-of-the-week names** are built from the planet names plus *-vāra* (day): *Ravivāra* (Sunday), *Somavāra* (Monday), *Maṅgalavāra* (Tuesday), *Budhavāra* (Wednesday), *Guruvāra* (Thursday), *Śukravāra* (Friday), *Śanivāra* (Saturday). This is the same **planetary-to-weekday mapping** preserved from the Mesopotamian system via Hellenistic astrology — remarkable parallel structure across cultures.
