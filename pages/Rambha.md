# Rambhā (रम्भा)

> *"She who shines with delight — and the plant that stands like her"*

---

## Amarakośa Definition

From **Amarakośa 2.4.322**:

> *kadalī vāraṇabusā **rambhā** mochā aṃśumatphalā*

Five synonyms for the **banana plant** (Musa sapientum):
kadalī, vāraṇabusā, **rambhā**, mochā, aṃśumatphalā

---

## Two Meanings of Rambhā

The word carries two distinct but poetically intertwined usages:

### 1. The Banana Plant (रम्भा)

The banana plant — its smooth, pale, cylindrical pseudostem
(a false trunk of tightly packed leaf sheaths).

**Etymology:**

| Component | Root | Dhātupāṭha | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **rambh / rabh** | √rabh | *ārambhe* — to begin, to grip | to hold firmly, to be stiff |
| **-ā** | feminine suffix | — | feminine noun marker |

- **√rabh / √rambh** = to grip, to hold, to be firm and upright
- The banana's pseudostem embodies this perfectly — firm, upright, smooth columns rising from the earth
- The suffix **-ā** makes it feminine, as the plant is classified *strīliṅga*

---

### 2. The Celestial Apsarā (रम्भा)

Rambhā is the most celebrated of the apsarasas — the archetypal beauty of Svarga, belonging to Kubera's court.

**Etymology (as apsarā name):**

| Component | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **ram** | √ram | to delight, to play, to be pleased |
| **-bhā** | √bhā | to shine, to be radiant |

- **√ram** (*krīḍāyām* — to sport, to delight) + **√bhā** (*dīptau* — to shine)
- = *"she who shines with delight"* or *"radiant with pleasure"*
- Appears in Rāmāyaṇa, Mahābhārata, and Purāṇas as the foremost among apsarasas

---

## The Poetic Convergence

In Sanskrit kāvya, the banana's smooth tapering trunk was the
**classical simile for a woman's thighs**:

> *rambhā-stambha-sama-ūrū* — "thighs like banana columns"

This kenning appears across Kālidāsa, Bhāravi, and Māgha.
So the apsarā Rambhā and the banana plant rambhā are
not accidentally sharing a name — both embody the same
quality of graceful, luminous, upright beauty.

One is rooted and fixed in the earth.
The other flows eternally through the heavens.
Yet they share one name — the poets seem to be saying
that even what appears firm and beautiful is, at root,
the same flowing impermanence.

## Rambhā in the Tradition

### Rāmāyaṇa
- Rambhā is accosted by Rāvaṇa — her husband Nalakūbara
  (son of Kubera) curses Rāvaṇa that if he touches any
  woman without her consent, his head will split into seven pieces
- This curse restrains Rāvaṇa from touching Sītā directly

### Mahābhārata
- Rambhā is sent by Indra to distract the sage Viśvāmitra
  from his tapas — she fails, and Viśvāmitra curses her
  to become stone for 10,000 years

### Devī Bhāgavata Purāṇa
- Book 6 describes the apsarasas as emanations of the Devī's
  *hlādinī śakti* (the power of bliss) — Rambhā chief among them
- They are not merely beautiful beings but expressions of
  *ānanda* (bliss) taking form — the Devī's own delight
  manifesting as beauty in the three worlds
- The Devī Bhāgavata frames all apsarasas as aspects of
  Mahāmāyā — the Great Illusion that enchants even the gods

## Etymology of the Banana's Other Names (Amarakośa 2.4.322)

| Name | Root / Meaning |
|---|---|
| **kadalī** | from *kadala* — the plant that grows in clusters |
| **vāraṇabusā** | *vāraṇa* (elephant) + *busā* (fodder) — elephant's food |
| **rambhā** | √rabh — firm, upright (the pseudostem quality) |
| **mochā** | from √muc — that which releases/liberates (the fruit separates easily) |
| **aṃśumatphalā** | *aṃśu* (rays/fibers) + *mat* (having) + *phalā* (fruit) — the fibrous-fruited one |
